Early counterfeiting was largely a physical craft---clippers would shave metal from genuine coins, and later, once paper notes arrived, forgers reproduced designs by hand. The shift to digital printing changed everything. A consumer-grade inkjet and a flatbed scanner are now sufficient to produce a fake note that most people cannot distinguish by eye alone. Central banks have responded by embedding increasingly sophisticated security elements: intaglio-raised text, polymer substrates, colour-shifting inks, and UV-fluorescent threads. Yet these measures have not eliminated the problem. The United States Secret Service, for instance, intercepts hundreds of millions of dollars in forged bills every year~. In Bangladesh, a single 2016 enforcement operation across Dhaka and Narayanganj recovered more than 6.6 million BDT in counterfeit 1,000 Taka notes~. High-end fakes now routinely pass a quick visual check, entering circulation through smaller retail outlets and informal markets where scrutiny is limited. The tools currently available to most businesses are simply not keeping pace. Basic UV lamps, still the most common frontline check, miss digital forgeries that replicate fibre patterns. Industrial sorting machines used by large banks are accurate but cost far too much for small businesses or rural branch offices to acquire. There is, in short, a genuine need for a detection approach that is both affordable and technically sound. To address this gap, we designed a multi-stage pipeline that combines three image-processing modules---OCR text verification, LBPH-based watermark recognition, and Hough-transform UV-line detection---and evaluated the system on the Bangladeshi 1,000 Taka note. Section~ summarises findings and identifies directions for future work. Research on automated banknote verification goes back several decades. Over time, methods have shifted from hand-engineered image features to deep neural networks, and each generation has brought measurable gains in accuracy---often at the cost of greater hardware demands. We survey this progression below to establish where our work sits and what gap it fills. The first wave of automated detection systems worked by extracting handcrafted features from scanned images. Hassanpour et al.~ used spatial frequency decomposition to characterise note texture, reaching 98\ One study combined texture data with intensity statistics, achieving approximately 96\ Deep learning changed the picture considerably. Rather than relying on engineers to define what features matter, CNNs learn directly from raw image data---and the results showed it. Ali et al.~ trained a convolutional network across a large multi-currency corpus and achieved 97.5\ Taken together, these studies reveal a clear pattern: the highest-performing systems all require either large curated datasets, GPU-class hardware, or both. What is notably absent from the literature is a system designed around the constraints of a small branch office in a developing economy---limited budget, standard laptop hardware, and notes that have been folded, handled, and worn in real circulation. The Bangladeshi Taka, despite being a frequently forged currency, has received very little dedicated attention. Our work targets this gap directly, accepting a modest accuracy trade-off in exchange for a system that can realistically be deployed without specialist infrastructure.
